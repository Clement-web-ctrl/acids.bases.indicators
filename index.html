INDICATORS.

An indicator is a substance that gives a definite color in acids and a different definite color in bases.

There are three types of indicators.

1.Simple acid base indicators
2.Commercial indicators
3.Universal indicators

Preparation of simple acid indicators.

Crush coloured leaves,flowers or roots in a motor using a pestel, add propanone,continue crushing and filter to obtain a thick color paste.The resulting paste is a simple acid-base indicator which can be confirmed by adding a few drops of it in a basic solution and other drops in an acidic solution, and it will give a distinct color in base and a distinct color in an acid.

.The disadvantage of this indicator is that they give inconsistent results.
.They expire within a short time.

These are commercially prepared and they are the ones commonly used in school laboratory as they give consistent results and do not expire easily.The commonly used indicators are shown below;

pH Scale and universal indicator

pH scale is a numerical scale ranging from 0-14 used to specify the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution.

Universal indicator is a mixture of indicators.It exhibits a range of colours in acids and in bases depending on the strength of the acid or base.When drops of universal indicator are added to a solution ,the colour obtained is compared with that of pH chart to determine the pH of the solution.

Advantage of universal indicator over other indicators.

It gives information of whether a substance is a strong or a weak base/acid.

How to determine pH of substances using universal indicator.

You can also be told to describe how to test the pH of a solid or a liquid.The procedure below is used to determine the pH of a substance.

If it is a solid:Add distilled water and stir to dissolve the solid.Dip the universal indicator paper or add 2 drops of universal indicator solution to a sample of the resulting solution then match the colour with the pH chart.

If it is a liquid:Dip the universal indicator paper or add 2 drops of universal indicator solution to the liquid then match the colour with the pH chart.

ACIDS

Definition:An acid is a substance that dissolves in water to form hydrogen ions as the only positive ions.

An acid can also be defined as a proton donor.

Acidity is the measure of hydrogen ions in a solution, hence hydrogen ions give a substance its acidic properties.

Properties of acids

Physical properties of acids

1.Acids have a characteristic sour taste.

2.Most acids are colourless liquids.

3.Mineral acids are odourless.Organic acids have characteristic smell.

4.All acids have pH less than 7.

5.All acids turn blue litmus paper red, methyl orange pink and phenolphthalein colourless.

6.All acids dissolve in water to form an acidic solution.

7.Have a scorching effect.

Chemical properties of acids

a)Reaction with metals

Dilute acids react with metals to form saltand hydrogen gas.The reaction is accompanied by effervescence due to the hydrogen gas produced.

The name of the salt is derived from the acid used.

Acid Salt produced
Sulphuric(VI) acid Sulphate
Nitric(V) acid Nitrate
Carbonic acid Carbonate
Phosphorous(V)acid Phosphate
Ethanoic/acetic acid Ethanoate/acetate
Citric acid Citrate
Hydrochloric acid Chloride

Magnesium + hydrochloric acid -magnesium chloride + hydrogen gas

Calcium + nitric (V) acid - calcium nitrate + hydrogen gas

Sodium + ethanoic acid - sodium ethanoate + hydrogen gas

Aluminium + hydrochloric acid - aluminium chloride + hydrogen gas

N.B

Very reactive metals like sodium and potassium react violently with acids and should never be tried in a school experiment.

The reaction between calcium and lead metals with dilute sulphuric (VI) acid slows down and eventually stops due to the formation of insoluble calcium sulphate and lead(II) sulphate respectively which coats the surface of the metal preventing reaction between the acid and the metal.

The reaction between lead metal and hydrochloric acid also slows down and eventually stops due to formation of insoluble lead(II)chloride which coats the surface of the metal preventing further reaction between the acid and the metal.

Copper metal does not react with dilute acids because copper is below hydrogen in the reactivity series hence cannot displace hydrogen in acid.

a)Reaction with bases

Dilute acids react with bases to form salt and water and the reaction is known as neutralization

Sodium hydroxide + hydrochloric acid - sodium chloride + water

Sodium oxide + nitric(V)acid - sodium nitrate + water

c)Reaction with carbonates and hydrogen carbonates

Acids react with carbonates/hydrogen carbonates to form salt, carbon(IV)oxide and water. This reaction is accompanied by effervescence and evolution of an odourless gas that forms a white precipitate with calcium hydroxide solution.

Note:

Strong acids completely ionize in solution.

Weak acids partially ionize in solution.

Uses of acids

Sulphuric (VI) acid is used as an electrolyte in car batteries/lead accumulators.

Dilute sulphuric (VI) acid and dilute hydrochloric acid are used to clean metal surfaces.

Weak acids are used to treat insect stings e.g wasp bites.

Nitric (V) acid is used in making fertilizers and explosives.

Concentrated sulphuric (VI) acid is used as a drying agent for gases that do not react with it .

BASES

Definition: A base is a substance that dissolves in water to form hydroxide ions as the only negative ions. It is defined as a proton acceptor.

An alkali is soluble base.

Properties of bases

Physical properties of bases

1.They have a slippery/soapy feel.

2.They have a bitter taste.

3.Solutions of bases are good conductors of electricity.

Chemical properties of bases

1.They turn red litmus paper blue.

Reaction with dilute acids

They react with acids to form salt and water as the only products.

Thermal decomposition

Most metals are decomposed by heat to form oxides and water.

Uses of bases

1.To manufacture toothpaste.

2.Used to manufacture anti-acid and ingestion tablets e.g magnesium hydroxide.

3.Sodium hydroxide is used to manufacture soap, paper and cement.

4.Calcium oxide and hydroxide is used to reduce soil acidity and manufacture of cement.

5.Bases are used to treat insect bites like ant and bee stings.

Nature of oxides

This refers to whether the oxide is acidic,neutral,basic or amphoteric.

1.Acidic oxides- these are usually non-metal oxides that dissolve in water to form acidic solutions e.g sulphuric (IV) oxide, caron (IV) oxide, sulphur (VI) oxide,nitrogen (IV) oxide e. t.c

2.Basic oxides- these are usually metal oxides that react with dilute acids to form salt and water only. E.g Sodium oxide, Magnesium oxide, Potassium oxide, Calcium oxide e.t.c

3.Neutral oxide:- these are oxides that have no effect on red and blue litmus paper, and they do not react with acids or bases. E.g Carbon(II)oxide, Nitrogen(I)oxide, Nitrogen(II)oxide, water(oxide of hydrogen)

4.Amphoteric oxides:- these are oxides that have the characteristics of both acids and bases.E.g Aluminium oxide, zinc oxide and lead(II)oxide. They react with both acids and bases.
